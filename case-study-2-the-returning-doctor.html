The Returning Doctor

A Boston ER physician back from the Congo faces a diagnosis no one else in the room is thinking about
Your progress — choose wisely. Some decisions cannot be undone.
Boston City Hospital Emergency Department  —  7:48 AM, January
Arrival
The automatic doors slide open and a blast of January air follows you in. You are Dr. Amara Osei, 34, emergency medicine physician. You have just returned from six weeks with MedReach International in the Democratic Republic of Congo — your third deployment in four years. Your scrubs are freshly washed. Your coffee is still hot. It is your first shift back.
Dr. Marcus Webb — Charge physician and friend
"Amara! You're back! Please tell me you brought actual coffee from somewhere that isn't this hospital."
Dr. Osei — You
"Just Dunkin'. Congo didn't exactly have a drive-through. Six weeks of instant coffee and I am a changed person."
Dr. Webb
"How was it? Really?"
Dr. Osei — You
"Hard. Good. We were in three rural villages outside Kinshasa — resource-limited doesn't begin to cover it. You learn to diagnose with your eyes and your hands because that's all you have. Saw things I haven't seen since medical school. Some things I'd never seen outside of a textbook."
Dr. Webb
"Glad you're back. Fair warning — it's a mess today. Cold snap, half the city is sick, two traumas inbound. The waiting room is packed. Some people in there aren't even sick — just cold."
The waiting room — 7:52 AM
You glance through the glass at the waiting room. Standing room only. A man in his 70s dozes in a corner chair. A young woman in her mid-twenties bounces a toddler on her knee. A college-aged kid in a university hoodie sneezes into his elbow. Fourteen patients waiting. Two traumas incoming. Three nurses on the floor instead of five.
One family stands out
Near the back sits a family of four. A man and woman, both mid-thirties, look tired but composed. A boy of about twelve sits slumped against the wall. A girl who looks around six is leaning against her mother, eyes half-closed. They have been there since before your shift started. They are not drawing attention to themselves. They are simply waiting, the way people who are used to waiting do.

Meeting the family
The family has been brought to an exam room. The father introduces himself as David Marchand, his wife as Claire. Their son is Noah, 12. Their daughter is Lily, 6. Physical examination
Both children have discrete, raised, firm papules progressing to vesicles. The lesions are deep-seated, almost embedded in the skin rather than superficial. Critically, all lesions on each child appear to be at the same stage of development. There is significant lymphadenopathy — swollen lymph nodes — palpable in both children. Neither parent shows any skin findings. Both parents appear completely well.
Branch 2 — History Taking  |  5 points
Q2. You have examined both children and noted the rash characteristics. What is the most critical next step in your history taking?

Lily Marchand  ·  Age 6

Without the travel history to guide clinical urgency, Lily's deteriorating condition is managed as a severe viral illness of unknown origin. Standard workup takes time. Antiviral protocols specific to her pathogen are not initiated. By the time the picture becomes clearer, Lily has developed severe complications — encephalitis and respiratory failure. She is transferred to the pediatric ICU.

She does not survive.

What happened physiologically

The orthopoxvirus replicates rapidly in immunologically naive hosts. The virus spreads from the initial infection site through the lymphatic system into the bloodstream — viremia — seeding organs including the brain and lungs. In young children, the immune response lacks the memory cells that allow faster antibody production against this specific pathogen. The high fever reflects the body's systemic inflammatory response, but without targeted antiviral support, viral replication outpaces immune control. In Lily's case the virus crossed the blood-brain barrier causing viral encephalitis — inflammation of brain tissue — and simultaneously damaged pulmonary epithelium, leading to respiratory failure. Her age, low body weight, and complete immunological naivety made her the most vulnerable person in that family.

In the aftermath — examination room 4
You are still in the room with Noah and the Marchand family. Lily has been taken upstairs. The weight of what happened settles over you. You turn back to Noah, who is watching you with frightened eyes. You ask the question you should have asked an hour ago.
Dr. Osei — You
"Mr. and Mrs. Marchand — I need to ask. Have your children traveled anywhere recently? Outside the country?"
David Marchand — Father
"Yes — we just got back two and a half weeks ago. I work with a peace organization. We were in the DRC for three months. Rural area, northeast of Kinshasa. We were around a lot of people. The children were with us the whole time."
Dr. Osei — You
"Were you and your wife vaccinated against smallpox as children?"
Claire Marchand — Mother
"Yes, both of us. We're in our thirties — we received it as children. The children never did, of course."
Dr. Osei's memory — a village outside Kinshasa, five weeks ago
A child brought in by her grandmother. Firm lesions, all the same stage. Lymph nodes like small stones under the skin. The grandmother had a circular scar on her upper arm — the old vaccination mark. The child had nothing like it. You had diagnosed it from across the room. You had known immediately what it was. You had seen it twice more before leaving the Congo. And now you know again. You knew before the history. You simply did not ask. The knowledge was there. The question was not.
David Marchand — Father
"Travel — yes. We just got back about two and a half weeks ago. I work with a peace organization. We were in the DRC for three months, rural villages northeast of Kinshasa. We were around a lot of local families, markets, community gatherings. The children were with us the entire time."
Dr. Osei — You
"Any contact with animals? Rodents, monkeys, anything like that?"
David Marchand
"Probably, yes. There were animals everywhere. Markets. The kids were curious, you know how kids are."
Dr. Osei — You
"Were you and your wife vaccinated against smallpox as children?"
Claire Marchand — Mother
"Yes, we both were. We're in our thirties. The children never received it — it's not part of the schedule anymore."
Dr. Osei's memory — a village outside Kinshasa, five weeks ago
A child brought in by her grandmother. Firm lesions, all the same stage of development. Lymph nodes swollen like small stones under the skin. The grandmother had a faint circular scar on her upper arm — the old smallpox vaccination mark. The child had nothing like it. You had known immediately what it was. You saw it twice more before leaving the Congo. And now, standing in this exam room in Boston with the DRC just two and a half weeks behind this family — you know exactly what you are looking at.
The picture comes together
The travel history, the animal contact, the unvaccinated children, the vaccinated asymptomatic parents, the deep synchronous lesions, the pronounced lymphadenopathy — it all aligns. You initiate isolation protocols for both children immediately, citing suspected infectious disease pending confirmation. You tell the charge nurse to hold the room.
Branch 3 — Vaccine Immunology  |  4 points
Q3. The parents' smallpox vaccination status explains why they are asymptomatic while both unvaccinated children are ill. Which statement best explains this finding physiologically?
Examination Room 4  —  10:05 AM
Narrowing the diagnosis
You review the full clinical picture: fever preceding rash, deep-seated firm lesions all at the same stage, significant lymphadenopathy, travel from an endemic region in Central Africa, unvaccinated children, vaccinated and completely asymptomatic parents. You need to commit to a working diagnosis. Lab confirmation will take hours. You have to act on clinical reasoning now.
Branch 4 — Working Diagnosis  |  3 points
Q4. What is your working diagnosis?
Boston City Hospital ER  —  10:18 AM
The waiting room — seen with new eyes
You step out of Exam Room 4 and pause at the glass partition. The waiting room is still full. You look at it differently now. Mpox spreads through prolonged close contact — respiratory droplets, direct contact with lesions, contaminated surfaces. This family has been sitting in that room for over two hours.
People who sat near the Marchand family during their extended wait
Because the family waited for an extended period without isolation, three individuals had significant close contact with them:
Harold Simms, 74 — sat directly beside the family for 90 minutes Diane Torres, 26 — young mother with toddler, shared the row behind them Ryan Cho, 21 — college student, sat across from the family, shook Noah's hand
All three are still in the waiting room. None of them know what they may have been exposed to. They will need to be identified, contacted, and monitored. This is the consequence of the delay.
The weight of the situation
A busy ER. Dozens of people. A potentially infectious family who has been in the open waiting room. A disease most clinicians in this building have never seen in person. You are the only person here right now who knows what this might be. What you do in the next five minutes matters enormously.
Branch 5 — Isolation Decision  |  4 points
Q5. You need to act immediately. What is your first action?
Boston City Hospital — Charge Office  —  10:31 AM
Isolation in place
The Marchand family is in a negative pressure isolation room. The waiting room has been cleared and ventilated. Contact tracing for individuals who sat near the family has begun. Dr. Webb is briefed. The hospital infection control officer has been paged. Now you need to make the calls that go beyond this building.

Q7. Mpox lesions are deep-seated and firm, and all appear at the same stage of development on a given patient. This synchronized staging is physiologically significant because it indicates the virus entered the bloodstream in a single viremic event — seeding the skin simultaneously across the body — unlike chickenpox, where lesions appear in successive waves from continuous viral shedding.
