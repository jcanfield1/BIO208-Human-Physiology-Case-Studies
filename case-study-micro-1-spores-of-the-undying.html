Phase 1 of 6  —  The Sealed World
You are the organism
You have been here for three thousand years.

You do not experience time the way a living animal does. You do not wait impatiently. You do not hunger in any way that causes suffering. You simply persist — metabolizing slowly in the darkness, breaking down what is available, producing spores when conditions allow, maintaining the colony across centuries with the quiet indifference of something that does not know it is extraordinary.

The tomb is your world. It has everything you need. Linen wrappings on a desiccated body — cellulose and protein, years of slow nutrition. Wooden coffin panels — more cellulose, complex polysaccharides. Pigments on the walls made with organic binders. Residual moisture trapped in the sealed space when the last human closed the final door and walked away into a world that has since risen and fallen several times over.

You are a fungus. An unknown isolate. You have adapted to this specific environment over hundreds of generations. You are very good at being here.
Your biological reality
The tomb has maintained a stable temperature of approximately 20°C, relative humidity of 60–70%, and complete darkness for three millennia. The organic substrates available to you — cellulose from linen and wood, proteins from mummified tissue residue, complex lipids from resin and oil offerings — have supported slow but continuous growth. The sealed environment has eliminated competition from most bacterial species and other fungi that require different conditions.

Phase 2 of 6  —  The Breach
The world opens
Something changes.

You have no sensory apparatus in the way animals do. You do not hear the sound of stone being moved. You do not see the light that splits the darkness for the first time in three thousand years. But you feel the change the way all biological systems feel environmental change — through chemistry, through physics, through the sudden and dramatic shift in the conditions that have defined your entire existence.

The air pressure changes. The humidity drops slightly. Then rises. A current moves through the chamber where there has never been a current. The temperature fluctuates. These changes — trivial to a human standing in the doorway squinting at the painted walls — are to you what an earthquake is to a building. Everything that was stable is now dynamic. Everything that was predictable is now uncertain.

Your biology responds. It cannot do otherwise. This is what you do.

Phase 3 of 6  —  Entry
A new world. Warm. Hostile. Alive.
You are inhaled.

The transition is absolute. One moment you are suspended in the cool dry air of the tomb, drifting in the disturbed currents. The next you are in something warm and wet and alive — a respiratory tract lined with mucus and cilia and immune surveillance that has been doing this specific job, detecting and destroying exactly what you are, for millions of years of host evolution.

You are 3 micrometers in diameter. You are a spore. You have no active motility. You go where the airflow takes you. The question is not whether you want to establish here. The question is whether your biology is equal to the challenge of this environment.
The host environment — what you encounter
The human respiratory tract presents a layered series of biological defenses. The upper airways are lined with ciliated epithelium covered in mucus — a physical and chemical barrier designed to trap and expel particles. The temperature is 37°C — significantly warmer than your tomb environment. The lower airways and alveolar space are patrolled by alveolar macrophages — professional phagocytes capable of recognizing and destroying fungal spores through pattern recognition receptors. The pH is tightly regulated. The oxygen tension is high. Everything about this environment is actively hostile to your establishment.

The Escalation  —  an event outside anyone's control
The sandstorm
You have been patient for three thousand years.

You can afford to be patient for thirty more minutes.

And then the wind comes.

It begins as a change in light — the sky above the excavation site turns the color of old copper, then amber, then a brown so deep it looks almost solid. Someone in the camp shouts. Equipment is grabbed. Tent flaps are tied down. The archaeologists who were working outside begin moving toward shelter with the particular urgency of people who have seen this before.

The field laboratory is a canvas tent with aluminum poles and mesh ventilation panels designed for airflow in the heat. It is not designed for a sandstorm. When the wind arrives in force — 80 kilometers per hour, carrying 3,000 years of desert particulate — the mesh panels become irrelevant. The canvas walls billow inward. The aluminum frame shudders. And the boundary between inside and outside, between controlled and uncontrolled, between contained and dispersed, dissolves in approximately four minutes.

On the laboratory bench inside that tent: sample vials from the tomb. Swabs in transport media. Open petri dishes that someone had not yet sealed because they were mid-process when the alarm was raised. Culture preparations. Slide preparations. Spore suspensions in buffer that a graduate student had been preparing for microscopy.

The wind that moves through that tent does not distinguish between sample and air. It carries everything it touches.

You are in that wind now. All of you. Spores from the tomb. Spores from the cultures. Spores from the surfaces of every imperfectly sealed container. You are distributed across the camp in a dispersal event that would have taken person-to-person transmission weeks to accomplish.

The twenty-three people at this excavation site have all just been exposed. Not all of them will develop infection — most healthy immune systems will clear the exposure. But the biology does not know that yet. The biology only knows that you are everywhere now.
▲ Organism status reset to Established regardless of prior performance. The sandstorm is not a consequence of any decision. It is biology encountering weather. Every student continues from this point with the organism in an Established state across the full camp population.
Phase 4 of 6  —  The Immune War
They know you are here
The initial encounter is over. You are established in lung tissue — at least some of you are. The sandstorm has scattered the rest of you across the camp. In the original host, the immune response is now fully mobilized. In the new hosts exposed during the storm, innate surveillance is just beginning to respond to your presence.

This is the phase that determines everything. The immune system has millions of years of evolutionary experience dealing with exactly what you are. You have millions of years of evolutionary experience evading exactly what it does. The outcome is not predetermined.
A complication in the original host
During the post-storm medical assessment of the camp, it is discovered that the lead archaeologist — the first person to enter the tomb and the individual with the highest initial spore exposure — has been taking oral corticosteroids for an autoimmune condition. This information was not disclosed during the pre-expedition health screening. The implications for your establishment in this particular host are significant.

Phase 5 of 6  —  Spread or Containment
What you have become
The sandstorm scattered you. The immune systems of twenty-two of the twenty-three exposed hosts are now making decisions about you — decisions that will be made at the cellular level, silently, without any awareness on your part. In the immunocompromised host you are growing. In the others, your fate is being determined by the strength of innate defenses you have been fighting since you first entered the respiratory tract.

The question now is not whether you will be eliminated in some hosts. You will be. The question is whether your biology — your spore structure, your thermotolerance, your immune evasion toolkit — is sufficient to establish disease in enough hosts to matter.

Phase 6 of 6  —  Identification
They are looking at you now
A researcher is preparing a culture from a bronchoalveolar lavage sample taken from the lead archaeologist. She has plated the sample on Sabouraud dextrose agar and incubated at 37°C. She is looking at you — or rather, at your colony — under a dissecting microscope. She has a compound microscope beside her and a reference text open on the bench.

You do not experience being identified. You do not know that what she sees determines what happens next. You are simply growing, doing what you do, expressing the biological characteristics that are intrinsic to what you are. But those characteristics — the color of your colony surface, the texture, the rate of growth, the microscopic structure of your conidiophores and conidia — are about to tell her everything she needs to know.
