The Weight of Trust

An ER doctor, a patient in crisis, and a physician he tried to stop years ago
Your progress
City General Hospital Emergency Department  —  Tuesday, 11:22 PM
Who you are
You are Dr. John Wylie, 48, emergency medicine physician. You have worked this ER for nineteen years. You have been offered department chief twice and declined both times. You are exactly where you want to be — at the bedside, in the room, in the middle of things. The ER at eleven on a Tuesday is a particular kind of busy: not chaos, just the steady accumulation of people who waited too long or had nowhere else to go. You are on your fourth hour of a twelve-hour shift when the ambulance bay doors open.
11:22 PM — bay three
Abby is 31 years old and she arrives conscious, which is something. She is on a stretcher with both hands pressed against her abdomen, breathing in short controlled pulls the way people breathe when breathing hurts. Her face is the color of old paper. Her husband Noah is beside her — he came in the ambulance, he refused not to — and he is holding her hand with both of his and looking at you with the particular expression of someone who has been frightened for hours and is now terrified.

"She's been in pain since this afternoon," he says before you can ask. "It kept getting worse. She's pregnant — eight weeks — she thinks she's losing the baby. Please."

Initial assessment
Abby's abdomen is visibly distended and exquisitely tender on palpation. She winces and pulls away when you press even gently on her lower quadrants. There is no rebound tenderness suggesting perforation but the degree of distension is significant. She is hypotensive and tachycardic — her body is compensating for something. Bedside ultrasound shows free fluid in the pelvis and abdomen. The intrauterine pregnancy is visible. There is a fetal heartbeat. For now.
Multiple Choice
Q1. Abby is hypotensive, tachycardic, and has significant abdominal distension with free pelvic fluid on ultrasound. She is eight weeks pregnant and has been in worsening pain since this afternoon. What is the most likely physiological explanation for the free fluid and hemodynamic instability?
Bay three  —  11:38 PM
Taking the history
You sit on the edge of the stretcher so Abby does not have to raise her voice. Noah is on her other side. She is more stable now — IV fluids running, pain management started — but still frightened. She answers your questions carefully, like someone who has been through enough medical appointments to know that the details matter.
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Tell me about the pregnancy. Was this a natural conception?"
Abby
"No. We've been trying for four years. I had — my care wasn't managed well when I was younger. Some scarring. We were told conceiving naturally would be very difficult."
Noah
"We found a specialist about eight months ago. He started her on a protocol — gonadotropins for stimulation, and then this newer drug, it's called Velitrin. It's expensive. I took a second job. We did two failed cycles and then this one worked. She was improving. We thought — " He stops. He looks at his wife. "We thought we were finally getting somewhere."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Who is your specialist?"
Abby
"Dr. Gregory Marsh. He's been — he's been very involved. He really believed this would work."
A name you know
You write the name on the chart. You read it back. You write it again. Gregory Marsh. You tell the nurse to contact Dr. Marsh and ask him to come to the hospital. You keep your voice even. You finish the assessment. You do not say anything else about it yet.

ER corridor  —  12:14 AM
John's memory — a patient room, years ago
A woman in her seventies describing her chest pain. The specific way she described it — not pressure, more like something pulling — a detail that mattered. Greg Marsh had been standing at the foot of the bed in his short white coat with his phone in his hand, thumb moving. John had said his name once, quietly. Greg had looked up and smiled and pocketed the phone and looked at the patient with an expression of attention that John recognized immediately as performed. His eyes were already somewhere else. Already in whatever lab or paper or project was more interesting than this room and this woman and the detail she was trying to give them.

Afterward John had gone to the program director. He had documentation — three separate incidents, a pattern, a patient who had asked not to have the student in her room again. He had said: this person is a clinical danger. He had been told that Greg's academic record was excellent, that one attending's concern was insufficient without a sustained documented pattern, that these decisions required more than a subjective assessment. Greg had passed his ER rotation. John had signed nothing he was comfortable signing. He had never forgotten it.
12:17 AM — the ambulance bay doors
He comes through the doors in a jacket over a dress shirt, no tie, the look of someone who got dressed quickly from somewhere comfortable. He is in his mid-thirties and he looks successful in the specific way people look when they have built something they are proud of — confident posture, good shoes, the easy authority of someone who is used to being in charge of his own space.

He sees John at the nurses' station and stops.

John sees him and does not move.

It is immediate and mutual and there is a full second where neither of them says anything and the ER noise continues around them as if nothing significant is happening.
Dr. Gregory Marsh
"Dr. Wylie. I didn't — I didn't know you were here." A beat. Then the recovery, smooth and practiced: "It's good to see you. It's been a long time."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Your patient is in bay three. Walk with me."
Dr. Gregory Marsh
"Of course. I've been following her progress closely — she was doing really well on the Velitrin protocol. Her estradiol levels were — " He is walking beside you now, talking with the confidence of someone who wants to be seen as competent. "I actually just presented her case at a symposium last month. The outcomes data on Velitrin has been really promising. I know it's expensive but the company has been very supportive — "
Dr. John Wylie — You
"I know about the company."
Dr. Gregory Marsh — a slight pause
"It's a legitimate partnership. I believe in the drug. I've seen it work."
Dr. Gregory Marsh — continuing, shifting
"You know I went into OB/GYN. Built my own practice — three physicians, we just expanded to a second location. Married, two kids. Things have gone well." A glance at you. "You're still in the ER."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Yes."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Your patient is in severe OHSS. She is hypotensive and tachycardic. There is significant free fluid in her abdomen and pelvis. She is eight weeks pregnant and her hemodynamic status is threatening that pregnancy. Her husband has been working two jobs to pay for a drug you are being compensated to promote, and that drug has been used off-label in her protocol. I need you to tell me everything about her treatment course. All of it."
Multiple Choice
Q4. Velitrin is being used off-label as part of Abby's fertility protocol. Dr. Marsh is a paid speaker for the pharmaceutical company that manufactures it. What is the primary ethical concern with this situation?
Bay three  —  12:45 AM
Abby's condition — hour two
Abby's blood pressure has responded partially to fluid resuscitation — she is at 104/68 now, still borderline. The fetal heartbeat remains present. Her abdomen is still distended and painful. The clinical picture is consistent with severe OHSS complicated by early pregnancy, which is the worst version of this syndrome — hCG produced by the early placenta acts as an additional gonadotropin signal, amplifying the VEGF-driven vascular permeability that is causing the fluid shifts. The pregnancy itself is making the OHSS worse.

Consultation room  —  1:15 AM
Alone with Greg
You have stepped out of the bay with Greg while the nurse monitors Abby. Noah is in the room with his wife. You have a narrow window and you use it. You tell Greg everything you know about Velitrin — the off-label application, the pharmaceutical payments, the absence of long-term safety data for this specific protocol in patients with Abby's history. Greg listens. He does not flinch. He leans against the wall with his arms crossed and he gives you the look of a man who has anticipated this conversation.
Dr. Gregory Marsh
"I hear what you're saying. But she was doing well. Her estradiol levels were within range. I monitored her closely. OHSS is a known risk of any stimulation protocol — it's not exclusive to Velitrin. This could have happened on a standard gonadotropin cycle."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"It could have. And you know as well as I do that the Velitrin adjunct increases VEGF expression by a mechanism that has not been fully characterized in early pregnancy. The company's own phase two data flagged it. Did you discuss that with Abby and Noah?"
Dr. Gregory Marsh — a pause
"I discussed the risks of ovarian stimulation."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"That is not the same thing."
Dr. Gregory Marsh
"I believe in this drug, John. I've seen it work. That couple has been through years of — they needed something that would actually work and I gave them that. She's pregnant. The pregnancy is still viable."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"For now."
Multiple Choice
Q8. Dr. Marsh states that he "discussed the risks of ovarian stimulation" but did not specifically discuss the additional risks associated with the off-label Velitrin adjunct. From an informed consent standpoint, what is the significance of this distinction?
True / False
Q9. A physician's genuine belief that a treatment will benefit a patient is sufficient ethical justification for prescribing a drug off-label when a financial relationship exists between that physician and the drug's manufacturer.
Bay three  —  1:44 AM
The decision to tell them
You make the decision in the corridor outside the bay. Abby and Noah have the right to know. They have been making decisions based on incomplete information — financial decisions, medical decisions, decisions about their future — and the incompleteness was not accidental. You go back into the room. Greg follows. He does not stop you. He stands at the foot of the bed with his hands at his sides and for the first time tonight he looks like what he is: a man waiting to face a consequence he helped create.
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Abby. Noah. I need to tell you something about the drug you've been taking, and I need to tell you now while we're making decisions about your care. It's important and it's going to be hard to hear."
Abby — her voice very quiet
"Tell me."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Velitrin was prescribed as part of your protocol off-label — meaning for a use that hasn't been fully approved or studied in patients with your specific history. Dr. Marsh has a financial relationship with the company that makes it. He is a paid speaker for them. I'm not telling you he didn't believe it would help you. I think he did believe that. But there is a conflict of interest that you were not told about, and there are risks specific to this drug that were not fully disclosed to you. You deserved to know both of those things before you made this decision."
What happens in that room
Abby does not say anything for a long moment. Then she begins to cry — not quietly, not the controlled kind of crying people do in medical settings to appear composed. Real crying, the kind that comes from a place where hope and loss exist at the same time and there is no dignified way to hold both. She covers her face with both hands. Her shoulders shake. Noah reaches for her and then stops himself and looks at Greg — looks at him the way a man looks at someone who has taken something from him — and when he speaks his voice is very steady in the particular way that means he is keeping himself from something worse.

"We trusted you," he says. Not loudly. Not dramatically. Just the fact of it, stated plainly, which is somehow the worst possible way to say it. "We gave you everything we had. I worked two jobs. She went through two failed cycles. We trusted you and you were taking money from the company that made the drug you were giving her. We didn't know that. We didn't know that." He looks at the floor. "We should have known that."
Multiple Choice
Q10. Dr. Wylie made the decision to inform Abby and Noah about the pharmaceutical conflict of interest in real time, while Abby was still being stabilized. What is the ethical basis for this decision?
City General Hospital  —  the hours that follow
Abby's condition — 4:30 AM
By four in the morning Abby is more stable. Blood pressure holding at 112/72. Heart rate down to 96. The free fluid is still present but has not increased. The fetal heartbeat is still there — measured, visible, persistent. She will be admitted to the OB/GYN service for monitoring. The OHSS will resolve over days to weeks as the pregnancy continues and the ovarian response gradually settles. The thromboembolic prophylaxis has been started. She is not out of danger but she is holding.
What is uncertain
The Velitrin adjunct's effects on the developing embryo during the period of maximum OHSS are not fully characterized. The drug's VEGF-modulating mechanism affects vascular development — and vascular development is critical in early embryogenesis. The company's phase two data had flagged a signal that had not been fully investigated before the drug was advanced. Nobody knows yet what that signal means for a baby born to a mother who received Velitrin during early pregnancy and then experienced severe OHSS. The pregnancy will continue. Abby will be monitored carefully. The baby will be watched. But the answer to what this did — if it did anything — will not come for months. Maybe longer.
Multiple Choice
Q11. The Velitrin adjunct affects VEGF signaling, and VEGF plays a critical role in early embryonic vascular development. Why is this physiologically concerning for the developing baby?
The corridor  —  5:10 AM
You find Greg in the corridor outside Abby's new room on the OB floor. He has been there for an hour. He looks like a man who has been sitting with something that does not get lighter the longer you sit with it. You stand beside him and for a moment neither of you says anything. Down the hall a nurse is making notes at a station. The fluorescent lights do what fluorescent lights always do at five in the morning.
Dr. Gregory Marsh — quietly
"I really thought it would help her."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"I know you did."
Dr. Gregory Marsh
"Does that count for anything?"
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Not to Noah. Not right now." A pause. "Greg. I tried to stop you from getting into clinical practice. Fifteen years ago. I raised concerns with your program director. You probably didn't know that."
Dr. Gregory Marsh — very still
"No. I didn't know that."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"I'm telling you now because I think you should know the full picture. And because I need you to understand that what I'm about to tell you is not personal. You should not be practicing clinical medicine. You were not suited to it as a student and the things that made you unsuited to it then are the things that led you to this corridor tonight. I am going to file a report with the hospital and the medical board. You should speak to an attorney."
Dr. Gregory Marsh — after a long silence
"You were always certain about everything."
Dr. John Wylie — You
"Not everything. But this."

Q13. The fact that Dr. Marsh genuinely believed Velitrin would help Abby, and that the pregnancy is currently viable, means the ethical and reporting concerns about his prescribing practice and pharmaceutical relationship are less serious than they would be if the outcome had been worse.
What John carries out of this night
He files the report at 6:15 AM from the nurses' station. It takes eleven minutes. He writes it carefully, the way he has always done things — completely, without editorializing, with the facts in order and the timeline clear. He has done everything he can do tonight. The report will go where it goes. The process will be what it is. He knows better than most people how these processes work and how slowly they move and how often they produce less than they should. He files it anyway. He filed it fifteen years ago and nobody listened and he files it again tonight because that is the only thing a person of integrity can do — keep doing the right thing even when the system does not make it easy and even when it comes too late to prevent the harm it might have prevented.

At 6:40 AM he looks in on Abby. She is asleep. Noah is in the chair beside her, head back, not quite asleep. The monitor beside the bed shows two heartbeats — hers and the baby's. John stands in the doorway for a moment and does not go in. They do not need him right now. They have each other and they have the monitors and they have the uncertain months ahead of them and they do not need him standing in the doorway.

He goes back to the ER. There are people waiting.
