Phase 1 of 5  —  The nurse's office, 9:15 AM
Setting the scene
It is a cold Tuesday morning in February. The windows at Maplewood School have been sealed shut since last week due to freezing temperatures. You are the school nurse. By 9:15 AM, three 7th-grade students have come to your office within 30 minutes of each other — all complaining of headaches and feeling "off." One says she feels slightly dizzy. There is a big science test scheduled for 3rd period today.
Student 1 — Jaylen, age 12
"My head is pounding. I don't feel good. I really don't want to go back to class."
Student 2 — Priya, age 13
"I feel kind of dizzy and a little nauseous. It started when I was sitting in homeroom."
Student 3 — Marcus, age 12
"I have a really bad headache. My eyes feel weird. Can I just lie down?"
Your observations
All three students appear alert but uncomfortable. No fever in any of them. No rashes, no vomiting. Skin color looks normal at first glance. Onset of symptoms in all three was within the last hour, in the same classroom.

Phase 5 of 5  —  Resolution and physiological analysis
11:15 AM — outcome
The school has been evacuated. Emergency services arrived and confirmed elevated CO levels traced to an improperly reconnected flue pipe on the boiler after Friday's maintenance. Within 20 minutes outside in fresh air, most students report their headaches fading and energy returning. No students required hospitalization. The nurse's early recognition and decisive action prevented a potentially life-threatening situation.
