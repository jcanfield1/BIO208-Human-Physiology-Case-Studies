Human Physiology — Undergraduate Nursing | Case Study 3 of 5

The Cliff

Two medical school graduates on a trip to reset. One of them is carrying something he doesn't fully know about yet.
Your progress — choose wisely. Some decisions cannot be undone.
Durmitor National Park, Montenegro  —  Day 9 of the trip
Where you are
You are Kevin Chen, 26, freshly graduated from medical school. You want to specialize in rare diagnostic medicine — the cases nobody can figure out, the ones that sit in charts for years before someone finally sees the pattern. You and your best friend Andrew Hale have been traveling for nine days through the Balkans. Croatia first, then Bosnia, now Montenegro. The plan was simple: stop thinking about medicine for a month. Stop thinking about residency applications and shelf exams and what comes next. Just be somewhere extraordinary and let the pressure out.
The monastery complex — morning
You have joined a small guided tour of the Ostrog Monastery complex, carved directly into a white limestone cliff face two thousand feet above the Zeta River valley. The view from the upper terrace is genuinely staggering — canyon walls, pine forests, a river that looks silver from this height. The other travelers on the tour are a mix of Europeans and backpackers. Marco Rossi, 26, an Italian long-distance runner. Dani Voss, 24, a German rock climber. Priya Nair, 27, a British ecology graduate student. A few others. Good people. The kind of travelers who move through the world quietly and carefully.
The climb up — 10:40 AM
The path to the upper monastery is steep and uneven — carved stone steps, some sections just packed earth. The morning is warm for October. You and Andrew have your packs on. Andrew sets a fast pace the way he always does, first up everything, competitive even when there is nothing to compete for. About two thirds of the way up, you notice him slow. His right hand goes briefly to his chest — just for a second, almost a gesture. He keeps moving immediately. His breathing is harder than the climb seems to warrant. He does not say anything.
Branch 1 — First Subtle Symptom  |  2 points
Q1. You notice Andrew's hand go briefly to his chest and his breathing is harder than the climb warrants. He does not acknowledge it. What do you do?
Outside the upper monastery  —  6:20 PM
The view at the end of the day
The tour group has mostly gone back down. You and Andrew are sitting on a low stone wall outside the upper chapel, backs against warm limestone, watching the light go out of the canyon. The Zeta valley is filling with shadow. The river is invisible now, just a sound far below. You have been here for twenty minutes not saying very much. After nine months of clinical rotations and board exams and twelve-hour days on wards where everything felt urgent, the silence is almost disorienting.

Andrew has his elbows on his knees, turning his water bottle in his hands. You know him well enough to know when he is about to say something real.
Andrew Hale
"Can I ask you something? What are you actually afraid of? Not residency stuff. Not the Match. Actually afraid of."
Kevin Chen — You
"That I chose the wrong thing. That I spent six years becoming something I don't actually want to be."
Andrew Hale
"Yeah." He is quiet for a moment. "I keep waiting to feel like a doctor. Like something clicked. It hasn't yet."
Kevin Chen — You
"Maybe it clicks when someone actually needs you."
Andrew Hale
"Maybe." He takes a long drink of water. Then, offhandedly, the way you mention things you have been carrying for a while: "Hey — you ever just feel your heart do something weird? Like a flutter. Just for a second and then it's gone."
Kevin Chen — You
"Sometimes. Yeah. Why?"
Andrew Hale
"I don't know. It happens sometimes. Not often. I read something once in Braunwald's — one of those conditions they describe as underdiagnosed in young people. I remember thinking it was interesting. But it always passes. It's probably nothing. We've been running on four hours of sleep and bad coffee for six years — everything feels weird."
Kevin Chen — You
"True. I had palpitations all through third year. Thought I was developing every condition I was studying."
Branch 2 — The Conversation  |  3 points
Q2. Andrew has just told you, casually, that he experiences cardiac flutter and mentioned reading about an underdiagnosed condition in young people. You feel palpitations too and the stress explanation is genuinely plausible. What do you do?
Andrew Hale — after you press him
"Mostly when I'm doing something physical. Climbing, running. Sometimes it comes with this weird dizzy feeling — like the world tilts for a second. Maybe twice a year, maybe less. I always just waited for it to pass and it did. I figured if it was serious it would have shown up by now."
Kevin's clinical memory — exertion-related palpitations with presyncope
Exertion-triggered. Brief. Accompanied by dizziness. Happens and then resolves. You are two years out from your cardiology rotation and the words surface slowly — outflow obstruction, ventricular hypertrophy, dynamic obstruction that worsens with exertion and dehydration. Young people. Often asymptomatic for years. You remember a sentence about it being the leading cause of sudden cardiac death in athletes under 35. You file it. You do not say it out loud. It is probably nothing. It has always been nothing. But you file it.
The cliff ledge  —  next morning, 9:55 AM
Off the path
The tour group is inside the lower monastery chapel. You and Andrew have wandered away — further than you should have — along a narrow path that runs outside the main complex and eventually gives way to bare limestone shelf. There is no railing. The cliff drops away on your left into the canyon several hundred feet below. The view is extraordinary. Andrew is ahead of you, photographing a carved stone cross embedded directly into the rock face, leaning slightly over the edge to get the angle.
9:57 AM — the collapse
Andrew straightens up from the shot. He turns toward you. His expression changes — not pain exactly, more like confusion, like he has forgotten what he was about to say. He puts his hand out toward the rock face. Then his legs go and he is on the ground.

He does not fall off the ledge. He slides down against the cliff face and lands on his side on the narrow shelf of rock, maybe eighteen inches from the drop. He is breathing. You can see his chest moving. But he is unconscious.

The tour group is inside a stone building out of earshot. Your phone has one bar. The path back is rough and uneven. You are alone with him on a ledge on a cliff face in Montenegro and your best friend is unconscious on the ground.
Branch 3 — Immediate Response  |  3 points
Q3. Andrew is unconscious on a narrow cliff ledge eighteen inches from a several-hundred-foot drop. He is breathing. What is your immediate action?
The cliff ledge  —  10:08 AM
Consequence of sitting Andrew upright
Andrew comes around briefly when you sit him up — his eyes open, he says your name — but almost immediately his colour drains and he loses consciousness again. In hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, sitting upright reduces venous return to the heart, which decreases left ventricular filling volume. In a heart with thickened walls and dynamic outflow obstruction, reduced filling worsens obstruction and can trigger further arrhythmia. His body is telling you clearly: flat is better than upright. You lay him back down carefully. You have cost him time and stability. The other travelers are arriving now.
Help arrives
Priya Nair heard you shouting and came first, moving fast and carefully along the narrow path. Marco and Dani are behind her. They stop when they see Andrew on the ground. Marco goes pale. Dani immediately looks at the terrain — she is a climber, she is reading the rock, thinking about how to move someone off it. Priya crouches beside you. Her Montserrat university hoodie says she is from London. She speaks clear English.
Priya Nair
"What happened? Did he fall?"
Kevin Chen — You
"He collapsed. No fall. He has a cardiac history — I think. I need to get him off this ledge and to a hospital but I need to move him carefully. Very carefully."
Dani Voss
"I have a first aid kit in my pack. And I know how to move someone on bad terrain. Tell me what you need."
Branch 4 — Moving Andrew  |  3 points
Q4. You have Dani, Marco, and Priya available to help. Andrew needs to be moved off the cliff ledge and down to the main path where a vehicle can reach. How do you direct them to move him?
The main path below the monastery  —  10:41 AM
Off the cliff
Andrew is off the ledge. Priya found a local man with a truck who is willing to drive — she negotiated it in halting Montenegrin and hand gestures and sheer determination. The nearest hospital with a cardiology department is in Nikšić, about forty minutes away on mountain roads. Andrew is conscious now, confused, frightened. He is asking what happened. You tell him he fainted. You do not say what you are thinking yet.
Consequence of the rushed move
The fast carry caused Andrew to regain consciousness abruptly in the middle of the move and become agitated and physically resistant, which spiked his heart rate and triggered a second brief syncopal episode. He is now conscious again but visibly more unstable — pale, diaphoretic, heart rate irregular. He will arrive at hospital in a worse state than he needed to. You have made the road ahead harder.
Kevin's phone — 10:44 AM
You try Andrew's parents. Thomas Hale — straight to voicemail. Susan Hale — same. You try twice more. Nothing. You stare at the phone for a moment and then call your father.
Robert Chen — Kevin's father
"Kevin? What's wrong — your mother said you called three times."
Kevin Chen — You
"Dad. Andrew collapsed. We're in Montenegro. I think it's cardiac. I can't reach his parents."
Robert Chen
"Okay. I'm listening. Tell me exactly what happened — symptoms, timeline, everything you observed. Your mother is already getting Thomas and Susan's number."
Branch 5 — The Phone Call  |  3 points
Q5. Your father has asked you to tell him exactly what happened. How do you respond?
In the truck, heading toward Nikšić  —  11:02 AM
The road
The truck is moving. Andrew is lying in the flatbed with his head in your lap. Dani is with you. Marco is in the cab helping with directions. Priya stayed behind to coordinate with the monastery and handle the tour operator. The mountain road switchbacks down through pine forest. Your phone rings. It is your father — and Thomas Hale is on the line.
Thomas Hale — Andrew's father — speaking fast
"Kevin — my God — is he breathing, is he okay right now — Susan, he's talking to Kevin — sorry, we just landed in Heathrow, we've been on a plane for nine hours, we had no signal — what happened exactly, is he conscious, Andrew was always healthy, always, he never — listen, he had a physical two years ago, everything was normal — Susan stop, I'm trying to — his uncle David, my brother, he died playing football, thirty-one years old, they said it was his heart but we never really understood what that meant, we didn't push, we should have pushed — is that relevant, does that matter, Kevin tell me if that matters."
Branch 6 — The Family History  |  3 points
Q6. Thomas Hale has just told you, almost in passing, that Andrew's uncle David died at 31 of an unexplained cardiac event during physical activity. How do you interpret this information?
General Hospital Nikšić  —  11:48 AM
Arrival
The hospital is a concrete building on the edge of a small city surrounded by mountains. The emergency staff speak very limited English — a junior nurse finds a colleague who can translate roughly. Andrew is on a gurney. He is conscious and frightened. A doctor — Dr. Milović, 50s, experienced — is examining him. She looks up at you with a question in her expression. She wants the history.
Dr. Milović — through the translating nurse
"You were with him. Tell me what you saw."
Branch 7 — Hospital Advocacy  |  3 points
Q7. Dr. Milović is asking for the history. You are a medical school graduate with a specific working diagnosis and a clear clinical picture. How do you respond?
General Hospital Nikšić  —  3:15 PM
The diagnosis
The ECG showed left ventricular hypertrophy with asymmetric septal thickening. The echocardiogram confirmed it — hypertrophic cardiomyopathy with dynamic left ventricular outflow tract obstruction. Dr. Milović finds you in the waiting room. Her English, it turns out, is better than she let on.
Dr. Milović
"Your friend has a condition he has had since birth. His heart muscle is too thick — here, in the wall between the chambers. When he exerts himself, when his heart beats fast and hard, the thickened wall can obstruct blood leaving the heart. This causes the dizziness. The palpitations. Today, the syncope. He was lucky. You were lucky you did not move him quickly." She pauses. "You knew what to look for."
Kevin Chen — You
"I wasn't sure. I had a hypothesis."
Dr. Milović
"In medicine, a good hypothesis at the right moment is worth more than certainty that arrives too late."
What happens next
Andrew is started on beta-blockers to reduce the obstruction and prevent arrhythmia. He is stable. Thomas and Susan Hale are on a flight to Podgorica. In four days Andrew will be cleared to fly home under medical supervision. He will be referred to a specialist in Boston. The diagnosis changes his life — no competitive athletics, regular cardiac monitoring, a conversation he will need to have with his future patients about family history. But he is alive. He is diagnosed. And the trip, Andrew says from his hospital bed with an IV in his arm and a heart monitor beeping beside him, was still worth it.
Physiological Understanding  |  2 points
Q8. Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy caused Andrew's syncopal episode because the thickened interventricular septum created dynamic outflow tract obstruction — meaning the obstruction worsens with increased heart rate, decreased ventricular filling volume, and physical exertion, all of which occurred simultaneously on the cliff.
